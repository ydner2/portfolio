The Global Endeavor: Implementing Design Thinking into Primary Education

STEM, DT, and the UN

The UN (United Nations) recently presented an issue that education globally leaves students unprepared for complex problems presented in the real world (2024, Yeung, WL., Ng, OL). Because surface level education does not always encourage problem solving, alternative education curriculums have started to develop. Two specifically are used often: STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics) and Design Thinking (DT) Education. In fact one thing STEM education does is help students “to think creatively and develop innovative solutions” (2024, Yeung, WL et al). Design Thinking (DT) is similar to STEM lessons but different. Design thinking is a way to counteract the complex problems that students are faced with helping students approach problems in a way that expands their horizons by helping them find an uncommon solution. Both terms can be educational subjects that are taught separately, but they are even more effective if they are taught together.

Globally, there has been an increase in Science, Technology, Engineering, and Mathematics, “STEM” education, with the addition of Design Thinking. Some countries that have explored DT and STEM education would be the USA, Turkey, China, Hong Kong, and Vietnam. But that does not mean that developing and implementing this new curriculum and way of teaching is without problems. In some countries STEM and DT education is almost non-existent. And in some countries there are not enough teachers with enough background knowledge in STEM and DT fields. Additionally because STEM education is uncommon, students have trouble understanding the new techniques and thinking in DT and STEM education. But why is design thinking and STEM classes essential in primary school?

How can design thinking improve the quality of education globally through problem-solving skills in primary school curriculum? There are many ways that design education can be incorporated into school systems globally, some are improvements to the curriculum, redesigning assessments, and even improvements to instruction–teaching the teachers so to speak, so let’s take a look at how different countries around the globe have incorporated STEM and DT Education and overcome the challenges that come with them.

STEM and Design Education around the Globe

The USA

One study in the USA in 2019 found that sketching instruction provided to students in primary school improved the overall design and also improved the comprehension, readability, and understandability found in the sketch (2019, Sung, E. et al). This study is older than most of the other case examples, (this might suggest that the USA is the foremost advocate for DT), it still offers us valuable insights. Specifically in this case study, students were tasked with a difficult electric engineering problem based on the design of a dog door (2019). The sketching instruction greatly improved the quality of the student designs compared to those without sketching instruction (2019). For example, the study showed that students without sketching instruction focused more on unnecessary aesthetic details (e.i. a doggy dog electric schematic does not need to have the entire house showing and taking up most of the page); whereas, students with sketching instructions drew accurate electrical schematics that would perform the task (2019). Notice the correlation between children’s problem solving abilities and the DT instruction provided. Perhaps sketching instruction could be provided as a way of thinking in language, writing, or even math, helping students to map out what is truly important instead of the unnecessary details.

Another study in the southern USA gave teachers “interdisciplinar” (e.i. English and Math together) design thinking kits (2020, Jocius, R.). One teacher remarked that students were better able to remember the lesson (2020). Notice the positive reaction another one of the teachers had:

“Lisa specifically described how the interdisciplinary elements of the kits supported student learning and allowed her to address multiple standards within one lesson: "I liked how one kit can literally do everything. I used it for math. I used it for reading. I used it for writing” (2020).

Design Thinking Education can therefore help students learn many courses effectively and efficiently. These kits also made learning “fun” and engaged students in the learning. In fact, the study found that students retained information better because they were better engaged (2020). Because design thinking helps students in the USA who is the foremost advocator in STEM and DT, it will definitely impact students globally, take Turkey for example.

Turkey

Some people might say that STEM education is just too different to incorporate in a national level of education. Introducing STEM into Turkey has proved difficult as incorporating STEM and Design Thinking (DT) into Turkish Curriculum is met with multiple problems. One of these difficulties is the lack of value in STEM from the government and from parents (2022, Öztürk, A., & Korkut, F.). Another difficulty is that there are not enough teachers with the expertise in Turkey or that there are some teachers but the curriculum is too complex for the age groups (2022). And there are many more difficulties in incorporating DT in the Turkish educational system as they found that the study presented many problems that teachers had to work around such as the National Assessments (2022). Even so, the DT in Vietnam benefited students by providing a way for the students to expand their thinking.

The Turkish study focused on incorporating DT into all subjects (interdisciplinary). The researchers introduced the teachers to DT and later tasked students to create solutions to a series of projects. In each successive study the teachers improved their teaching and the students engaged better in the learning (2022).

The study found that teachers were more capable of helping students be successful in design thinking when all of them had the training as various perspectives and fields of study offered insights into student behavior (2022). We could imagine how useful having a variety of different teachers was as you would be taught how to think like a designer in English, Math, Science, and other subjects. Furthermore, the Study found:

“Adopting the risk-taking [...] of the DT approach was found to be helpful for teachers when dealing with the students’ [...] helping them to leave their comfort zones in the shift from traditional education to STEM education” (2022, Ozturk).

Could you imagine the design thinking in every different subject, this would definitely help students to be able to solve the problems they will later face in the workforce. For example, Design Thinking in English could be drawing pictures to help students capture main events in their essay, or even in history creating an empathy map (to be discussed below) could help students imagine how people felt during historical events. What really does this study show? That design thinking and STEM education benefits students by helping both students and teachers think outside the box as they solve complex problems.

Hong Kong and China

Next we move to China. Similarly in Guangzhou China, students participated in a two month course focused on STEAM (STEM + Art) (2023, Zhan, Z.). The students were pre-tested and post-tested to their creative thinking aptitude (2023). During the study students were collaboratively tasked with accomplishing two design challenges. The study found the design instruction and projects promoted students' creative thinking, creativity aptitude, and empathy” (2023). From the study in China we learn that creative/design thinking and empathy both help students to engage better in their education and give students a way to understand and solve the problems.

We now travel to the port of Hong Kong. The study in Hong Kong was based in an afterschool enrichment program aimed to introduce students to DT. The study found that empathy maps, commonly found in the Empathizing or Brainstorming stage, helped middle school students produce results that would provide solutions to real world problems (2024, Yeung, WL., Ng, OL). Empathy maps (EM) (See Appendix) are design strategies that involve putting oneself in the shoes of target groups; in the case study there was a greatly improved problem solving design found in each student group that used EMs (2024). These empathy maps helped the students to not just look at the surface level solutions but to dig deeper and find out what their targeted audience “felt” or experienced in day to day life (2024). For example, after a design thinking educational course students were asked to imagine what a blind person felt, while the younger students did tend to give surface level answers, the students were better able to address the needs of the blind, elderly, and even lazy (2024). Again we see another DT strategy that helped the students understand and solve complex issues that one would face in the workforce.

Vietnam

Our final destination is Vietnam. In Vietnam the drive to include STEM education extensively in schools is clear: “The [Vietnam] government’s Project 146 aims for 80% of schools in Vietnam to incorporate STEM/STEAM education activities by 2030” (2024, Yeung, WL et al). This is unique because most of the above studies are based on experimenting with how STEM and DT instruction can fit into the education system; rather, in Vietnam, STEM education is a nationwide goal that is planning to implement the specialized education plans in 2030! Interestingly, this study commented on the different learning styles of the students and showed that the technology competence increased through the DT exercises (2024). Overall they concluded with the study with positivity looking forward to include STEAM (STEM + Art) in future studies and the educational system in the future (2024). The STEM and DT education in Vietnam and other countries will help students in the long run to solve problems at work, problems that finite questions on tests will not be able to help them with.

Conclusion

STEM and DT around the world has seen new growth as this new method of teaching difficult concepts has proved to be useful in this ever changing advancing society. While there are some problems with installing STEM education in specific countries, all show that STEM and DT instruction helps children to think outside the box and create solutions to complex problems. Ultimately, these different ways of teaching could lead to the solution of the UN’s call to improve education around the world.
